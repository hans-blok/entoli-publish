We prove first (then scale) ()
**Short description** We first prove that something has value, before trying to make it big. ### Rationale In an early stage, scale is often a distraction. The real risk is not that something fails to scale, but that it has no meaningful value. By focusing on value first, we learn what really matters and avoid wasting energy on scalability problems that may never become relevant. This principle guards our focus and prevents over-engineering and premature optimisation. It forces us to work with small, real signals rather than large assumptions. ### Implications **For product development**: - Ideas are first substantively tested with a small, concrete audience - We build for a concrete use case, not for "the market" in general - Small, real signals (usage, feedback) weigh more heavily than large assumptions **For architecture**: - Scalability follows from value, not the other way around - We build simple and extensible, not complex and "future-proof" - We refactor towards scale when it proves necessary, not in advance **For decision-making**: - Decisions are made based on learning and feedback, not on ambition or assumptions - We invest in experiments and prototypes before making large commitments - We accept that not every idea scales, and that is okay ### Examples **Good** ✓ - Testing a new agent first with one concrete use case and improving iteratively - Manually walking through a workflow before building automation - Publishing a knowledge article and gathering feedback before producing a whole series - A simple file-based implementation before introducing a database **Bad** ✗ - Immediately setting up a complex microservices architecture for a first prototype - Building a scalable database solution before knowing whether anyone uses the product - Developing a general framework without a concrete use case - Investing in performance optimisation before knowing whether the feature has value --- ## Coherence and Implementation These three principles reinforce each other: **Sovereignty** guards our independence and autonomy. **Architectural Thinking** guards our coherence and structure. **Value before scale** guards our focus and learning. Together they form a foundation for how we work: independent, structured and focused on real value. ### Conflicts between principles In practice, principles can sometimes clash: - Sovereignty can lead to more complexity (doing everything ourselves vs. buying) - Architectural Thinking can slow things down (structure takes time) - Value before scale can lead to technical debt (learning fast vs. building robustly) In such dilemmas: 1. Make the trade-off explicit 2. Deliberately choose which principle takes precedence in this specific situation 3. Document the choice and the rationale 4. Evaluate afterwards whether the choice was right
